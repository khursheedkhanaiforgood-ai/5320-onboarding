Key Lessons (Verbatim Sources)

Blue = Claude AI dialogue · Purple = NotebookLLM synthesis

"The secondary switch doesn't need to know HOW to route — it just needs to know WHERE to send frames. That's the L2 contract. SW1 owns all the L3 intelligence: gateway IPs, DHCP scopes, routing decisions. SW2 is just a really fast frame-switcher."
— Claude AI InterVLAN dialogue, April 21
"Karl Rule: The Default VLAN (VLAN 1) must have ipforwarding enabled on SW1. Without it, clients get IPs but have no internet — return traffic from the modem can't cross to data VLANs. This is the single most common deployment failure in EXOS primary/secondary setups."
— NotebookLLM Karl Lab v2 synthesis, April 21
"When a client on VLAN 10 pings a client on VLAN 20, the packet goes: Client A → SW2 (L2 forward to trunk) → SW1 (L3 routing decision, rewrites src/dst MAC to gateway MACs) → SW2 (L2 forward to destination port). The IPs never change. Only the MACs change at each L3 hop."
— Claude AI New Lab Karl dialogue, April 21
"XIQ handles what the CLI cannot: L3 firewall rules with IP Objects give you policy-based inter-VLAN blocking without touching EXOS ACL syntax. The division: SW1 CLI owns QoS marking and queue assignment. XIQ owns SSID-level enforcement, user profiles, and firewall rules."
— NotebookLLM Final Items (QoS for Claude), April 21
"The DHCP trunk reach-back is a classic EXOS gotcha. You configure DHCP on SW1 for VLAN 10 — but if you only enable it on port 5 (the wired access port), DHCP Discover packets arriving over port 10 (the uplink trunk from SW2) get dropped. You must include port 10 in the dhcp enable command."
— Claude AI Full Day session, April 21
"Guest-to-Guest isolation can't be solved with ACLs alone. ACLs operate at L3 — but two Guest devices on the same VLAN communicate directly at L2 (the switch just forwards frames). XIQ's 'Drop traffic between stations' firewall action is enforced on the AP itself, stopping the frames before they even hit the switch."
— NotebookLLM Karl Lab synthesis, April 21
"LLDP is on by default in EXOS. This is the carrier for Fabric Attach. Even without pings working, 'show lldp neighbors' gives you ground truth about what's physically connected and tagged. If your AP appears in lldp neighbors, the trunk is working."
— Claude AI InterVLAN dialogue, April 21
"OWE (Opportunistic Wireless Encryption) solves the open-network risk — but legacy devices can't connect to an OWE-only SSID. OWE Transition Mode solves this: run a hidden open SSID alongside the OWE SSID with the same name. OWE-capable devices connect encrypted; legacy devices fall back to the hidden open. XIQ exposes this as a single toggle."
— NotebookLLM Final Items synthesis, April 21

OWE — Backward Compatibility Answer
What is OWE Transition Mode?

OWE (Opportunistic Wireless Encryption, 802.11-2016/WPA3) encrypts traffic on open networks with no PSK required. The problem: legacy devices (older Android, pre-WiFi 6 Windows, IoT) don't recognize OWE and refuse to connect.

Solution: OWE Transition Mode — two SSIDs, one experience:

SSID TypeVisible?SecurityWho connects
OWE SSID (primary)YesOWE encryptedWPA3-capable devices
Open SSID (companion)HiddenNone (open)Legacy devices — auto fallback

XIQ location: Guest_Wireless SSID config → "Transition Mode for 2.4GHz and 5GHz" toggle. Enable it and XIQ broadcasts both SSIDs automatically.

Tradeoff: Legacy devices get zero encryption. OWE-capable devices get encryption with zero friction (no PSK). This is the standard enterprise approach during WPA3 transition.
